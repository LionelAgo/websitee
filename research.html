Research Focus Areas

Exploring the complexities of wall-bounded turbulence, flow control, and data-driven approaches in fluid mechanics.

Research Philosophy

My research is driven by a multidisciplinary approach that combines fundamental fluid mechanics, high-fidelity numerical simulations, and cutting-edge data science techniques. By integrating these fields, I seek to deepen our understanding of complex flow phenomena and develop innovative strategies for flow prediction and control.

With a focus on wall-bounded turbulence across a range of Reynolds numbers, my work addresses critical challenges in energy efficiency and environmental sustainability by targeting drag reduction and heat transfer enhancement in engineering systems.

The research methodologies I employ span from direct numerical simulations (DNS) and large eddy simulations (LES) to advanced machine learning algorithms, particularly auto-encoders for reduced-order modeling and feature extraction from turbulent flows.

Core Research Areas

Wall-bounded Turbulence

Investigating multi-scale interactions in turbulent boundary layers

Multi-scale Dynamics and Scale Interactions

My research in wall-bounded turbulence focuses on understanding the intricate dynamics of turbulent structures across multiple scales, particularly the interactions between large-scale outer structures and small-scale near-wall turbulence at high Reynolds numbers. These interactions become increasingly significant as Reynolds number increases, fundamentally altering the statistical characteristics of the flow and affecting key engineering quantities such as wall friction and heat transfer.

Through detailed analysis of direct numerical simulation data, I have developed novel methodologies for scale separation and analysis, enabling a deeper understanding of the modulation effects of large-scale structures on near-wall turbulence. This work has challenged existing paradigms, particularly the quasi-steady hypothesis, by revealing complex asymmetric responses and non-linear interactions that conventional models fail to capture.

Developed auto-encoder methodology to separate large and small scale turbulent motions in high Reynolds number flows
Demonstrated asymmetric modulation and "splatting" effects that standard models fail to account for
Established quantitative connections between turbulent structures and their contributions to wall shear stress
Examined scale-dependent phase relationships in turbulent boundary layers

Machine Learning Approach in Fluid Dynamics

A New Paradigm for Flow Analysis and Control

Traditional approaches to flow modeling and control face significant challenges when dealing with the high-dimensional, non-linear, and multi-scale nature of turbulent flows. My research leverages machine learning techniques to overcome these limitations, offering a transformative framework for studying complex fluid dynamics problems.

Rather than following the classical sequential process of understanding, modeling, and then optimizing, my approach employs an iterative methodology that couples data-driven modeling with optimization of control strategies. This paradigm shift enables more efficient discovery of effective control laws and deeper insights into flow physics.

The integration of physical knowledge with data-driven methods is a cornerstone of my research philosophy. By embedding physical constraints and domain expertise into machine learning architectures, I develop models that are both accurate and physically consistent, bridging the gap between purely data-driven approaches and traditional physics-based modeling.

1

Feature Extraction with Auto-Encoders

The first step involves using specialized auto-encoder architectures to extract the most relevant features from high-dimensional flow data. These neural networks compress the complex flow information into a low-dimensional latent space that captures the essential dynamics of the system. By analyzing this latent representation, we can identify coherent structures and dominant flow patterns that would be difficult to detect with traditional methods.

2

Clustering and Reduced-Order Modeling

The low-dimensional representation from the auto-encoder is further analyzed using clustering algorithms to identify distinct flow states and their transitions. This approach enables the construction of reduced-order dynamical models that can efficiently predict the temporal evolution of the flow. By combining the auto-encoder with clustering techniques, we obtain a powerful framework for understanding complex flow dynamics in a computationally efficient manner.

3

Physics-Informed Machine Learning

To enhance the reliability and physical consistency of the models, I incorporate domain knowledge and physical constraints into the machine learning algorithms. This physics-informed approach ensures that the predictions respect fundamental conservation laws and physical principles, leading to more robust and interpretable models. The integration of physical knowledge also improves the generalization capabilities of the models to conditions not seen in the training data.

4

Optimization and Control

The final step involves using the reduced-order models to design and optimize control strategies for specific objectives, such as drag reduction or heat transfer enhancement. Advanced optimization algorithms, including reinforcement learning, are employed to explore the parameter space efficiently and identify optimal control laws. This data-driven approach to flow control has demonstrated significant potential for discovering novel control strategies that outperform traditional methods.

Future Research Directions

Multi-scale Control of Wall Turbulence

My future research aims to develop innovative control strategies that operate simultaneously at different scales of turbulence, allowing for enhancement or limitation of heat transfer across a wide range of Reynolds numbers. This multi-scale approach will target both near-wall structures and large-scale outer motions, exploiting their interactions to achieve unprecedented control capabilities.

By leveraging the understanding of scale interactions gained from my previous work, I plan to design control algorithms that can adaptively respond to the multi-scale nature of the flow, providing more effective and robust performance than single-scale approaches. This research will have significant impact on renewable energy development and improvement of industrial processes using heat exchangers.

Hybrid Data-Physics Framework

I aim to develop a comprehensive methodological framework that integrates physical knowledge and data-driven approaches to design optimal active control strategies for complex fluid systems. This hybrid approach will combine the interpretability and generalizability of physics-based models with the flexibility and predictive power of machine learning algorithms.

The framework will incorporate multi-fidelity modeling, uncertainty quantification, and adaptive learning techniques to create robust and reliable models that can operate across a wide range of flow conditions. This approach will offer new possibilities for improving industrial processes, particularly in the context of complex geometries made possible by additive manufacturing.

Adaptive Modeling for Thermofluid Systems

Future research will focus on developing adaptive models capable of adjusting to changing flow conditions, enabling accurate prediction and robust control in variable industrial environments. These models will leverage online learning techniques and transfer learning approaches to continuously update their predictions based on new observations.

A key aspect of this research will be the integration of these adaptive models with sensing and actuation systems to create intelligent control frameworks that can optimize performance in real-time. Drag Reduction & Heat Transfer

Flow control through wall oscillations and turbulence manipulation

Breaking the Reynolds Analogy

My research on drag reduction and heat transfer employs spanwise wall oscillations as an active flow control strategy to manipulate near-wall turbulence. This work has led to significant advancements in our understanding of the physical mechanisms underlying drag reduction and, more recently, heat transfer enhancement in wall-bounded flows.

A breakthrough finding from our recent work is the demonstration of preferential enhancement of heat transfer over drag through carefully tuned wall oscillation parameters. This phenomenon effectively breaks the Reynolds analogy, which traditionally couples momentum and heat transfer processes. By showing that convective heat transfer can be improved by 15% while drag increases by only 7.7%, we've opened new possibilities for optimizing thermal management systems in engineering applications.

Revealed fundamental mechanisms of drag reduction through phase-wise analysis of oscillatory wall motion
Demonstrated the decoupling of heat and momentum transport (breaking the Reynolds analogy)
Analyzed statistical properties of turbulence under active control using joint probability density functions
Explored Reynolds number effects on control effectiveness

Machine Learning in Fluid Dynamics

Developing data-driven approaches for complex flow analysis and prediction

Auto-Encoders and Reduced-Order Modeling

My work in machine learning applications to fluid dynamics has pioneered innovative methodologies for analyzing, modeling, and predicting complex turbulent flows. I've developed specialized auto-encoder architectures to extract the coherent structures from high-dimensional flow fields and construct low-dimensional dynamical models that capture the essential physics of the flow.

By combining clustering algorithms with reduced-order representations from auto-encoders, I've established a framework that efficiently identifies the dominant flow patterns and their temporal evolution. This approach has been successfully applied to various flow configurations, including canonical wall-bounded turbulence and more complex geometries, demonstrating superior feature extraction capabilities compared to traditional decomposition methods.

Developed auto-encoder technology for feature extraction and reduced-order modeling of turbulent flows
Created clustering-based dynamical systems models from low-dimensional representations
Applied data-driven modeling to extract physical insights from complex flow fields
Integrated physical constraints into machine learning architectures for improved generalization

Shock-Wave Boundary Layer Interactions

Understanding complex interactions in high-speed flows

Unsteadiness in Separated Supersonic Flows

My early research focused on shock-wave boundary layer interactions (SWBLI), a critical phenomenon in high-speed aerodynamics that affects the performance and safety of supersonic and hypersonic vehicles. Through detailed numerical simulations and analysis, I investigated the mechanisms driving shock unsteadiness and the dynamics of flow separation in these complex interactions.

A key contribution of this work was the concept of "zones of influence," which provides a framework for understanding the regions that significantly impact shock motion and flow dynamics. This methodology has enabled more targeted approaches to flow control and prediction in high-speed aerodynamic applications.

Characterized the mechanisms driving low-frequency shock oscillations in separated flows
Developed the "zones of influence" concept for analyzing shock-boundary layer interactions
Studied the dynamics of separation bubble dilation and collapse in shock-induced separations
Investigated three-dimensional modulations in shock-induced separation
