Boundary Layer Theory

Auto-encoder-assisted analysis of amplitude and wavelength modulation of near-wall turbulence by outer large-scale structures in channel flow at friction Reynolds number of 5200

L. Agostini & M. A. Leschziner

Physics of Fluids, Vol. 34

2022

Show Abstract
This article presents a new methodology using an auto-encoder algorithm to separate large- and small-scale motions from extremely large direct numerical simulation fields of a channel flow at friction Reynolds number Reτ ≈ 5200. This approach overcomes the limitations of the empirical mode decomposition method previously used at lower Reynolds numbers. The modulation of small-scale turbulence by large-scale structures is quantified using multi-variable joint probability density functions and the derivative of the second-order structure function, providing insights into the underlying mechanisms. The validity of the quasi-steady hypothesis, proposing the universality of near-wall turbulence when scaled by the variable large-scale wall shear stress, is also examined.

On the departure of near-wall turbulence from the quasi-steady state

L. Agostini & M. A. Leschziner

Journal of Fluid Mechanics, Vol. 871

2019

Show Abstract
This study examines the validity of the quasi-steady hypothesis in representing the effects of large-scale outer motions on near-wall turbulence in boundary layers. Direct numerical simulation data from a channel flow at Reτ ≈ 1000 is analyzed to assess both amplitude and phase modulation effects. The research reveals limitations in the quasi-steady model, particularly in capturing the asymmetric response of near-wall turbulence to positive and negative large-scale fluctuations, and the phase relationships between scales. These findings have important implications for predictive modeling of wall-bounded turbulent flows at high Reynolds numbers.

Drag Reduction

Statistical analysis of outer large-scale/inner-layer interactions in channel flow subjected to oscillatory drag-reducing wall motion using a multiple-variable joint PDF methodology

L. Agostini & M. A. Leschziner

Journal of Fluid Mechanics, Vol. 923

2021

Show Abstract
This paper presents a statistical analysis of interactions between large-scale outer structures and small-scale near-wall turbulence in a channel flow subjected to spanwise oscillatory wall forcing, using a methodology based on multi-variable joint probability density functions. Direct numerical simulation data is analyzed by consolidating the dataset into joint PDFs, allowing the extraction of a wide range of conditional statistics. The study focuses on elucidating the mechanisms by which near-wall turbulence is modified by the action of large-scale footprints, which tend to strengthen as the Reynolds number increases. The strongly asymmetric response of the production rate and turbulence level in the buffer layer to positive and negative footprints is identified as a partial explanation for the diminishing effectiveness of drag-reducing wall motion as Reynolds number increases.

Heat Transfer

Preferential Enhancement of Convective Heat Transfer Over Drag Via Near-Wall Turbulence Manipulation Using Spanwise Wall Oscillations

L. Guérin, C. Flageul, L. Cordier, S. Grieu, & L. Agostini

International Journal of Heat and Fluid Flow, Vol. 1010:109564

2024

Show Abstract
This study examines the manipulation of convective heat transfer through spanwise wall oscillations in a turbulent channel flow. Direct numerical simulations are performed at Reτ = 180 and Pr = 1. The main objective is to explore the heat transfer response to oscillation parameters that promote drag increase, a regime that has received limited attention. By adopting a period (T+ = 500) and amplitude (W+ = 30) reported to increase drag, a remarkable dissimilarity between momentum and heat transport emerges. Under these conditions, convective heat transfer experiences a substantial increase of 15%, while drag increases by only 7.7%, effectively breaking the Reynolds analogy. A comprehensive statistical analysis elucidates the physical mechanisms responsible for this dissimilar behavior, examining the effect of control on near-wall streaks and associated momentum and heat mixing by analyzing energy distribution across scales and wall-normal positions. The investigation highlights the potential of using unconventional spanwise wall oscillation parameters to preferentially enhance convective heat transfer while minimizing the associated drag penalty.

Breaking the Reynolds Analogy: Decoupling Turbulent Heat and Momentum Transport via Spanwise Wall Oscillation in Wall-Bounded Flow

L. Guérin, C. Flageul, L. Cordier, S. Grieu, & L. Agostini

arXiv preprint, 2312.13002

2024

Show Abstract
This paper explores the decoupling of turbulent heat and momentum transport through spanwise wall oscillations in wall-bounded flows. The research challenges the traditional Reynolds analogy, which suggests a direct correlation between heat transfer and momentum transport in turbulent flows. By implementing strategic wall oscillation parameters, the study demonstrates how heat transfer can be enhanced more significantly than drag, providing new opportunities for thermal management in engineering applications. This breakthrough has important implications for the design of more efficient heat exchangers and thermal management systems.

Exploration and prediction of fluid dynamical systems using Auto-Encoder technology

L. Agostini

Physics of Fluids, Vol. 32(6):067103

2020

Show Abstract
This paper introduces a novel methodology employing auto-encoder technology for the exploration and prediction of fluid dynamical systems. The approach enables the identification of coherent structures in complex flow fields and the construction of low-dimensional dynamical models that capture the essential physics of the flow. By combining clustering algorithms with the reduced-order representation obtained from the auto-encoder, the method provides an effective framework for analyzing and predicting the behavior of complex fluid systems, with applications in flow control and optimization.

Show Abstract
